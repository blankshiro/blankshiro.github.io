# Meaning of Life(?)

Life is strange. If you stop and think about it for too long, it starts to feel like a bad joke—or maybe a cosmic prank. We’re born, we stumble around trying to figure out what’s going on, and then eventually, we die. In between, we try to make sense of everything: Why are we here? What’s the point? Does any of it actually matter?

>   Spoiler alert: probably not.

The truth is that life doesn’t come with a manual, a roadmap, or even a vague set of instructions. The universe doesn’t care about us or our plans. And the more you try to dig for some grand hidden meaning behind everything, the more you realize there might not be one. It’s an uncomfortable thought at first, but the more you sit with it, the more freeing it becomes.

------

## Everything Is Ridiculous (And That’s Okay)

Think about how absurd life really is. We’re tiny specks on a spinning rock, hurtling through space at unimaginable speeds. We make up stories about love, success, and happiness to keep ourselves entertained. We work jobs we don’t always love, chase goals that may or may not make us happy, and buy stuff to distract ourselves from the fact that we’re all going to end up in the same place anyway.

But here’s the kicker: none of it actually *has* to matter. The universe doesn’t owe us meaning. It doesn’t care if we get that promotion or fall in love or finally hit our 10,000 steps for the day. The sun is going to rise and set whether we figure out life or not.

*And maybe that’s the beauty of it.*

------

## The Myth of Meaning

If you’ve ever looked at your life and thought, *What’s the point?*, congratulations—you’re not alone. People have been asking that question for centuries. Some turn to religion, others to philosophy, and a lot of us just turn on Netflix and try not to think about it too much.

But the idea that life needs to have a "point" is kind of arbitrary, isn’t it? Why can’t we just *be*? Why does every moment, every action, every relationship have to mean something profound? Maybe the search for meaning is what makes life feel so heavy. Maybe life is lighter if we stop demanding it make sense.

------

## Finding Freedom in the Absurd

This is where nihilism comes in—the idea that life has no inherent meaning. At first glance, it sounds depressing. But hear me out: if nothing truly matters, then you don’t have to follow anyone else’s rules. You don’t have to live up to anyone’s expectations. You can let go of all the pressure to "be someone" or "leave a legacy."

When you stop worrying about the "bigger picture," you’re free to focus on the smaller things: the stuff that makes you happy right now. Maybe it’s eating your favourite meal, drinking your favourite coffee, or listening to music that gives you goosebumps. Those little moments of joy might not mean anything in the grand scheme of things, but who cares? They’re real. They’re yours. And that’s **enough**.

------

## Laughing at the Void

Philosopher Albert Camus had this great analogy about life. He compared us to Sisyphus, this guy from Greek mythology who’s cursed to roll a giant boulder up a hill, only to have it roll back down every time he gets close to the top. Forever. It’s a miserable situation, but Camus flips the script. He says we should imagine Sisyphus happy—because the struggle itself is enough. The act of pushing the boulder, knowing it’s pointless, is where the meaning lies.

It’s a wild idea, but it makes sense. If life is absurd, why not embrace the absurdity? Why not laugh at the fact that none of it makes sense? There’s a strange kind of freedom in just rolling with the randomness and finding joy where you can.

------

## So, What Now?

*I don’t know*. Here’s the thing: if life is meaningless, that means it’s up to *you* to decide what matters. There’s no cosmic scoreboard. No one’s keeping track. You get to define your own purpose—or not. You can take it seriously or treat it like the weird game it is. Either way, the only thing that really matters is how *you* feel about it.

Maybe the secret isn’t in finding some grand purpose but in learning to appreciate the absurdity of it all. Life doesn’t have to make sense to be worth living. It doesn’t have to be profound to be beautiful. Sometimes, it’s enough to just exist, to laugh, to love, and to push your boulder up the hill one more time.

So, here’s to the absurd. Here’s to the chaos. And here’s to finding joy in the meaningless mess of it all.
